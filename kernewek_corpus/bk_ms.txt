# `Bewnans Ke': the Life of St Ke 
# In April 2002 the world of Cornish studies was astonished and thrilled by 
# the news that a manuscript containing a new Middle Cornish text had been 
# discovered (Graham Thomas, `Two Middle Cornish plays: a note', National 
# Library of Wales Journal, 32 (2001 ), 121  2). The forthcoming edition of 
# the text by Graham Thomas and Nicholas Williams is eagerly awaited; 
# meanwhile many people, in Cornwall and elsewhere, are justly keen to know 
# more of the contents of National Library of Wales MS. 23,849D, for both its 
# linguistic and its literary interest. It is therefore desirable for a 
# reliable text to be made available as soon as possible, pending the 
# publication of Thomas's and Williams's edition. Thomas himself has 
# generously given his approval of my making an interim version of the text 
# available, until their edition is published. The National Library of Wales 
# has generously given permission for the text of its MS. 23,849D to be made 
# public in this way. All users of the present text will be grateful to Graham 
# Thomas and to the National Library for their generosity; once Thomas's and 
# Williams's printed edition is available, this internet-text will be removed. 
# Apparently nothing is known of the provenance of the manuscript. It was 
# found among the papers of the late Professor J. E. Caerwyn Williams after 
# his death in 1999. Caerwyn Williams seems never to have mentioned the piece 
# to anyone, although he had transcribed a small portion of it and must have 
# recognised its significance. The manuscript of the only other surviving 
# Middle Cornish saint's play, the Life of St Meriasek, is also in the 
# National Library of Wales, to which it came as part of the collection at 
# Peniarth, near Tywyn in the county of Merioneth (MS. Peniarth 105). Since 
# both these Cornish saints' plays, like the earlier Ordinalia (the Biblical 
# cycle), were evidently written at Glasney College at Penryn, it is possible 
# that the two manuscripts travelled to Wales together, and became separated 
# later. Many people, myself included, wondered at first whether this 
# discovery was too good to be true; but its genuineness seems guaranteed by 
# the script, by the language, and (as Andrew Hawke kindly informs me) by the 
# sixteenth-century watermark on the paper. The genesis of the present text 
# has been as follows. I transcribed it in summer 2002 from photographs 
# supplied by the National Library. Michael Polkinhorn checked my 
# transcription against his own, also made from photographs. I have sent a 
# copy of my text to Graham Thomas; the two transcripts have been compared, 
# and we have discussed the discrepancies. In a few places we have agreed to 
# differ as to the intended reading, but the text offered here is therefore 
# very close to Thomas's diplomatic text which will appear in Thomas's and 
# Williams's edition. Thomas has kindly supplied his numbering of the stanzas, 
# and they are the ones used here. They may be altered very slightly in the 
# final edition, and are therefore provisional at this stage. What is offered 
# here is essentially what is visible on the photographs; there are one or two 
# places where a letter (usually one easily deduced) is apparently visible on 
# the manuscript but not on the photographs. Although for the most part the 
# reading of the manuscript is clear, in a few awkward passages the 
# establishment of correct readings goes hand-in-hand with the elucidation of 
# the meaning, and in some of these passages it may be that a slightly 
# different reading will eventually come to be agreed. The following 
# conventions have been observed in the text given here. Since capital letters 
# and word-spacing are used inconsistently in the manuscript, as so often at 
# this period, I have standardised capital letters for proper names, according 
# to the sense where that is apparent to me at this stage. There will be many 
# other places where word-division or capitalisation will need to be altered 
# as elucidation proceeds. Since such work is very much in progress, I have 
# not presumed to punctuate the text. Expansions of abbreviations in the main 
# text have been indicated by italics, which are also used for the speakers, 
# the Latin stage-directions and the Cornish prose `headlines' (see below); 
# but it has not seemed necessary to indicate expanded abbreviations within 
# the stage-directions. Because the Arthurian section follows the story given 
# by Geoffrey of Monmouth so closely (see below), I have chosen forms 
# approximating to the conventional Galfridian ones for the names of some 
# minor kings whose names in the manuscript are written ambiguously (notably 
# Epistopus, corresponding to Geoffrey's Epistrophus, and Augelus). I have 
# occasionally offered an emendation, where there is no doubt what is 
# necessary, but in these cases I have indicated the manuscript reading as 
# well; such cases are ones where there is a simple scribal error of copying, 
# of a kind readily explained by the usual principles of textual criticism. 
# Other letters in square brackets have been supplied, either because they 
# were omitted by the scribe, or in places where the manuscript is damaged but 
# letters can safely be restored. The letters n and u, and sometimes other 
# letters, are ambiguously written in the manuscript, and only the sense can 
# dictate which should be used; there may well be places where elucidation 
# will show that I have chosen wrongly. No doubt there are also other minor 
# errors, typographical or other, still remaining, and I shall be very pleased 
# to incorporate any corrections sent to me. On the pages of the manuscript 
# the lines are laid out in a manner which indicates the metrical structure of 
# each stanza, and I have retained that indication by a system of up to three 
# indentations: lines whose indentation corresponds here are ones occupying 
# corresponding places within the rhyme-scheme of a stanza. The contents of 
# the play are given in a synopsis below. An old numbering of the leaves 
# indicates that the manuscript, which now contains twenty leaves, formerly 
# had five more leaves at the beginning, a pair of leaves at two points in the 
# interior (two between the present folios 8 & 9, and two between the 
# present 12 & 13), and presumably five more leaves at the end. These losses 
# affect our understanding of the contents. At first it was thought that the 
# manuscript contained two plays, one about St Ke (on the present 
# folios 1 - 8) and one about King Arthur (folios 9 - 20); but for reasons 
# given below it seems likely that the Arthurian section actually formed part 
# of the Life of St Ke, in which case the manuscript contains a single play, 
# and the lost leaves would have linked the two sections. The modern name 
# Bewnans Ke has accordingly become an agreed one for the whole text of the 
# manuscript, on the assumption that it is a single work, and by analogy with 
# Bewnans Meriasek, the modern name for the other saint's play in Middle 
# Cornish. However, St Ke is not mentioned in the extant Arthurian section. 
# The hand changes noticeably between the two sections, the earlier being in a 
# secretary hand of the mid-sixteenth century, while the later is in an italic 
# hand; but close examination suggests that the same person copied both parts, 
# since some scribal habits, and some letter-forms, are shared between the 
# first and second parts. However, the quality of the language and of the 
# dramatic action is considerably greater in the first section than in the 
# second. A moral saying spoken by St Ke in the first section is repeated 
# word-for-word by Modred in the second (stanzas 135 and 427). The key to 
# understanding the contents of the work is provided by a French summary of a 
# lost Latin life of St Ke, given by Albert Le Grand, in his La Vie, gestes, 
# mort, et miracles des saincts de la Bretagne armorique (Nantes, 1637, and 
# subsequent editions), under the saint's feast-day, 5th November. In the last 
# third of this summary the saint, after his dealings with King Theodoric 
# (Teudar) and the founding of his monastery at Kea, returns to Brittany. He 
# is then sent for by the prelates of Britain, asking him to intercede between 
# King Arthur and his nephew Modred, in an attempt to avert civil war. (The 
# story here follows very closely that of Geoffrey of Monmouth, and is 
# evidently derived from it, directly or indirectly.) However, with the 
# arrival of a Saxon army to aid Modred, St Ke decides that he can do nothing 
# to help and returns again to Brittany, passing through Winchester where he 
# persuades Queen Guinevere to enter a nunnery. He dies shortly afterwards at 
# Cleder, near the north coast of Finistere. (See also G. H. Doble, Four 
# Saints of the Fal (1929), especially pp. 7 - 21.) From this account it is 
# clear that we should expect a Life of St Ke to conclude with the story of 
# Arthur's betrayal by Modred, and it is therefore very probable that 
# Le Grand's summary thus explains the section concerning Arthur in our 
# manuscript. If so, we must suppose that in the two folios lost between 
# folios 8 & 9, St Ke returned to Cleder in Brittany, and the scene then 
# changed to King Arthur and his refusal to acknowledge the overlordship of 
# the Emperor Lucius in Rome; and likewise we must suppose that the presumed 
# five folios lost at the end of the manuscript included St Ke's abortive trip 
# to Britain, and then his return to Cleder and his death there. The language 
# of the text, like that of the Life of St Meriasek, is good Middle Cornish. 
# It largely fails to show any of the sound-changes by which Late Cornish (the 
# language of the late sixteenth to the eighteenth centuries) is defined, such 
# as the diagnostic change of nn to dn. (See O. J. Padel, A Popular Dictionary 
# of Cornish Place-Names (1988), pp. 26 and 32  4.) These sound-changes began 
# to appear frequently in the later sixteenth century, and are often shown in 
# the play The Creacion of the World (1611), though that text also continues 
# to use, by convention, some Middle Cornish spellings. Our scribe, though 
# writing presumably in the second half of the sixteenth century, twice 
# complained that his exemplar had been spoilt by the loss of pages (see the 
# remarks on folios 5r and 20r), so he was avowedly copying from an older 
# manuscript. We may conjecture that the lost original may have been written 
# in around 1500, like that of St Meriasek written in 1504, and the state of 
# the language would accord with this estimate. 
## O. J. Padel 
## ojp10@cam.ac.uk

## Summary of the action of `Bewnans Ke' 
# The numbers are those of stanzas (as supplied by Graham Thomas); conjectures 
# of plot come from the French summary of a lost Latin life of St Ke, found in 
# A. Le Grand, La Vie ... des saincts de la Bretagne armorique (Nantes, 1637; 
# and subsequent editions), 5 November. 

# [Five folios lost, presumably recounting St Ke's noble birth, education and 
# promotion to the episcopacy, which he later renounces.] 

# 1 - 11 ... ... St Ke [still in Brittany?] restores to life one of three 
# shepherds, and then embarks for Cornwall by boat. {Or according to the 
# Cornish text, by flying flag stone!! No Celtic saint should be without one}
# King Teudar's forester finds St Ke in the king's forest [of Rosewa, where Ke 
# had landed], and takes him to the king at Goodern [in Kea parish]. 

# 12 - 84 Teudar and Ke in theological dispute; Teudar orders St Ke to be 
# imprisoned; further disputation. Teudar agrees to give St Ke an estate near 
# the forest of Rosewa, and then plans to go hunting there. 

# 85 - 108 [Text incomplete, owing to damaged folios in the scribe's exemplar: 
# in the lost text St Ke shelters a stag hunted by Teudar, and Teudar or his 
# men (4 `torturers') steal Ke's oxen, and break three of his teeth when he 
# demands them back.] 

# 109 - 123 Ke causes a holy well to arise at Killiow Soor [in Kea parish]; he 
# cures a leper, who gives the saint more land [also in Kea parish]. Stags 
# come to plough for St Ke in place of his stolen oxen. 

# 124 - 180 Teudar [taken ill?] negotiates with Ke to make amends for his broken 
# teeth. Teudar and Ke agree that Ke shall have whatever land he can impark 
# while Teudar is in the bath; Teudar consults Owbra to concoct a potion; 
# Teudar gets stuck in the bath, and thus loses a large portion of land. 

# [Two folios lost: in the lost text St Ke returns to Cleder in Brittany?] 

# 181 - 230 Arthur in state: (181 - 192) Duke Cador and Augelus king of 
# Scotland; squires, bishops; (193 - 201) Beduer, Ke (Kay), Hoel, Cador, and 
# others; (205 - 212) 7 kings, as guests of Arthur, plus Modred and Gawain; 
# (216 - 230) knights. 

# 231 - 269 Emperor Lucius learns that Arthur refuses to pay tribute; he 
# sends 12 legates to Arthur (235 - 247, the 12 legates hail the emperor; 
# 255 - 269, they greet Arthur). 

# [Two folios lost] 

# 270 - 305 Arthur, with legates and kings, refuses to pay tribute; the 
# legates return to Lucius. 

# 306 - 355 Lucius sends for senators, kings, and dukes, who come to greet 
# him (339 - 353, 7 kings and 5 dukes greet Lucius). Lucius takes counsel and 
# departs for France. 

# 356 - 361 Arthur leaves Modred in charge and takes leave of Guinevere. 

# 362 - 374 Battle [in France] between Arthur's and Lucius's forces; Lucius is 
# killed and Arthur sends his head to Rome. 

# 375 - 409 Modred and Guinevere together in Arthur's palace; a bishop crowns 
# Modred. 

# 410 - 424 The news is brought to Arthur, who takes counsel. 

# 425 - 430 Modred obtains the help of the Saxon Cheldric. 

# 431 - 434 Battle between Arthur's and Modred's forces. 

# 435 - 437 Guinevere in the palace. 

# [Five folios lost? If the Cornish play followed the Latin life as summarised 
# by Le Grand, St Ke was presumably sent for as mediator but realised that his 
# mission was hopeless; he returned to Brittany, passing through Winchester 
# where he visited Guinevere and persuaded her to join a convent, and then 
# died peacefully in his community at Cleder.]
